1. The Invisible Tax on Every Grocery Receipt

When a supermarket manager decides to put ground coffee on a "Buy 2 Get 1 Free" promotion, the decision is almost always wrong. Not because promotions don't work - they do - but because the calculation underlying the decision ignores almost everything that matters. It ignores whether customers buying discounted ground coffee will simultaneously skip the premium instant coffee at full margin sitting three shelves away. It ignores whether the same basket that picks up the offer also buys filters, creamer, and mugs - halo effects worth more than the margin surrendered. And it almost certainly ignores that the same supplier promoted the identical SKU six weeks ago, training shoppers to wait rather than purchase at full price.

This isn't incompetence. It's an information architecture problem. Trade promotion decisions are made by people - often experienced, smart category managers - who are working with spreadsheets, disconnected ERP exports, and mental models built over years of pattern-matching. The actual causal graph of product relationships in a large grocery network has hundreds of thousands of edges. No spreadsheet contains it. No human mind can hold it.

What is trade promotion spend? Consumer goods companies and retailers allocate funds - sometimes called "trade funds" or "co-op dollars" - to in-store promotions: temporary price reductions, end-cap displays, feature advertising, and buy-one-get-one deals. CPG companies typically spend 15–25% of total revenue on trade promotion, making it the second-largest line item after cost of goods sold.

The scale of the problem is staggering. An estimated $500 billion is spent globally on CPG trade promotions every year, with some industry estimates placing the true figure closer to $1 trillion when all promotional mechanics are included. Between 37 and 40 percent of this spend, according to consistent findings from Nielsen, NielsenIQ, and CommerceIQ, delivers no measurable sales lift whatsoever. That's roughly $185–200 billion in promotional budget that evaporates annually into negative-ROI events, customer stockpiling, cannibalisation of adjacent SKUs, and post-promotion demand troughs.

The existing software market knows this problem exists. Enterprise vendors - SAP Trade Management, Oracle TPM, Anaplan, IRI, Exceedra - have built multi-hundred-million-dollar businesses selling solutions for it. SAP alone holds over 40% of TPM implementations at Fortune 500 CPG firms. The global trade promotion optimization software market reached $1.86 billion in 2024 and is growing at a 12.3% CAGR toward a projected $5.4 billion by 2033.

But here is the gap those vendors have collectively failed to close: almost every commercial TPO system still optimizes promotions in isolation, one SKU at a time. They are sophisticated regression and lift-modelling machines applied to silos. None of them natively represent the product relationship graph - the web of substitution relationships, co-purchase dependencies, and promotional interaction effects that determines whether a promotion helps or hurts the total category.

That is precisely what Promograph is built to address.

The Problem in Detail

2. Cannibalization, Halo, and the Network Effect Nobody Measures

Before understanding what Promograph does, it's worth being precise about the two effects that make promotion optimization a graph problem rather than a regression problem.

Cannibalization: The Substitution Tax

When product A is promoted, demand for product B - a close substitute - typically falls. A shopper who buys discounted ground coffee 200g may not buy the 500g bag they'd otherwise have purchased at full margin. In a supermarket that carries 40,000 SKUs, the substitution graph has extraordinary density. Academic research by Cohen et al. (2017, 2021) in Management Science demonstrates that promotion planning that ignores cross-item substitution effects systematically overestimates promotional ROI and recommends promotions that destroy rather than create category value. Their formal model - the Multi-POP (Multi-item Promotion Optimization Problem) - shows that accounting even for just pairwise (bilateral) interactions is sufficient to reach near-optimal solutions, but existing tools rarely do even this.

Halo Effects: The Co-Purchase Dividend

The converse of cannibalization. Promoting product A increases sales of product B if the two are systematically co-purchased. Promoting a recipe kit drives sauce sales. Promoting beer drives crisp sales. Promoting diapers drives baby wipes. These halo relationships are mined from basket transaction data and represent incremental revenue that a single-SKU model misses entirely - often enough to make a promotion that looks margin-negative in isolation actually margin-positive at the basket level.

Why This Is a Graph Problem

Both effects are fundamentally relational. They cannot be represented as properties of individual products; they live in the edges between products. A product with 50 substitute competitors and 30 co-purchase dependents has a completely different promotional profile than a product that is isolated in the graph - and that profile changes every time a new competitor launches or a seasonal relationship appears. You need a data structure that can represent, traverse, and reason over this relationship network dynamically. That data structure is a graph.

Academic Foundation

Cohen et al. (2021) in Management Science formally prove that for demand models with additive cross-item effects, accounting for bilateral (pairwise) interactions is sufficient to reach optimality. Their Multi-POP formulation is the academic ancestor of what Promograph implements at scale, with GNN embeddings providing the cross-item similarity structure that feeds the LightGBM forecaster.

How Promograph Works

3. Promograph: Architecture of a Graph-Steered Promotion Optimizer

Promograph is a six-layer system that answers a single, specific question: "What happens to network-wide revenue if I promote this product, at what discount, and for how long?" Each layer adds context the layers below it cannot provide alone. Together, they produce a recommendation that is simultaneously graph-aware, inventory-aware, margin-constrained, and explainable in natural language.

The architecture is deliberately stacked, not monolithic. You can engage each layer independently - which is why the entire system runs locally at zero cost, but slots cleanly into enterprise Databricks infrastructure with a single environment variable change.

The Key Engineering Insight: Typed SSE Events, Not Prose

The single most important design decision in Promograph is how the LLM communicates with the graph frontend. Most conversational AI systems parse the model's text output to extract entities and actions - a fragile, error-prone approach that breaks when the model phrases things differently.

Promograph inverts this. Every entity the assistant references is emitted as a typed Server-Sent Event, not prose. Three event types drive the entire interface:

# Typed SSE events emitted by the orchestrator during a reasoning turn graph_node → {id, type, label, metrics} graph_edge → {source, target, type, weight} recommendation → {sku, discount_depth, net_roi, margin_ok, rationale}

The React frontend renders the product graph purely from these events. It never parses the LLM's natural language output. Nodes flash into existence as the model works through its reasoning. Red nodes indicate cannibalized neighbours. Green nodes are halo beneficiaries. The graph is not a visualization of results - it is the reasoning process made visible.

And critically: a user can click any node to steer the next turn. That click is posted back as a first-class message with full conversational context, so the model can drill into the cannibalization pressure on that specific substitute, or the co-purchase strength of that halo neighbour. The graph is not static output. It is interactive input.

The GNN Engine

4. Why GraphSAGE? The Inductive Embedding Choice

The heart of Promograph's predictive capability is a GraphSAGE (Graph Sample and Aggregate) model trained over the product relationship graph. Understanding why this architecture was chosen over alternatives requires a brief detour into what makes retail product graphs unusual as graph ML problems.

The Inductive vs. Transductive Problem

Early GNNs - particularly the original GCN formulation by Kipf & Welling (2016) - are transductive: they produce embeddings only for nodes present during training. For a retail product graph, this is a fatal limitation. Supermarkets add new SKUs constantly. A seasonal product, a new supplier line, or a private-label launch all require immediate embedding so the system can assess their promotional interactions. A transductive model requires full retraining every time the product catalogue changes.

GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) is inductive. It learns aggregation functions over neighbourhood features, not node-specific parameters. A new product node gets an embedding by running its neighbourhood through the learned aggregators - no retraining required. This is not an academic nicety; for a production retail system with tens of thousands of SKUs and weekly catalogue changes, inductivity is a hard operational requirement.

What the Embeddings Capture

Each SKU embedding produced by the GraphSAGE encoder encodes, implicitly, the structural position of that product within the entire relationship network. Two products that are deep substitutes - same sub-commodity, similar price point, similar purchase frequency - will sit close together in embedding space even without explicit co-labelling. Two products that are distant in graph topology - no shared substitutes, no co-purchase relationships - will be far apart. This makes the embeddings informative features for a downstream forecasting model in a way that raw category labels or price features simply cannot be.

The Feature Pipeline

# Features fed to LightGBM forecaster (post-GraphSAGE) # --- Graph-derived features (from Neo4j subgraph) --- substitute_pressure # sum of edge weights to substitute neighbours halo_pull_strength # weighted co-purchase affinity to halo neighbours degree_centrality # how connected this SKU is in the graph promo_interaction_count # # of edges to other recently-promoted products # --- GNN embedding (64-dim GraphSAGE output) --- sku_embedding_0..63 # structural position in product topology # --- Classical tabular features --- baseline_velocity # units/week at full price (4-week rolling) price_elasticity # estimated from historical promo response inventory_weeks_cover # current stock / avg weekly velocity days_since_last_promo # post-promotion dip guard category_promo_density # # of other SKUs promoted in same category this week discount_depth # proposed % discount # --- Target variable --- net_incremental_margin # lift × promo_margin - cannibalization_loss + halo_gain

The critical feature here is net_incremental_margin as the target - not gross uplift. Most commercial TPO systems predict volume lift on the promoted SKU. Promograph predicts net incremental margin across the entire neighbourhood, which means the model is rewarded for finding promotions that generate basket-level value rather than single-SKU volume at the expense of adjacent full-margin sales. This target formulation is the difference between optimizing the wrong thing brilliantly and optimizing the right thing well.

The Pricing Agent

Once the GNN + LightGBM pipeline returns a net ROI estimate, the scipy pricing agent solves a constrained optimization problem over the discount depth dimension. The objective function maximizes net_incremental_margin + clearance_value (the latter scaled by inventory weeks cover, so overstocked items receive additional pressure to discount) subject to a hard margin floor constraint. This produces an explainable recommendation: a specific discount percentage with a rationale grounded in actual margin math, not a black-box output.

The Databricks Fit

5. Why This Fits the Databricks Ecosystem Like a Glove

Promograph was built to be genuinely useful as a standalone local tool. But its design choices - particularly the Genie client abstraction and the DuckDB ↔ Unity Catalog data flow - make it a near-perfect demonstration application for the Databricks Data Intelligence Platform. Understanding why requires mapping each layer of Promograph to the corresponding Databricks capability.

Genie Spaces as the Natural Language Data Layer

The most architecturally elegant piece of Promograph is the GenieClient abstraction. The interface is simple: GenieClient.ask(question) → GenieResult. The local MockGenieClient maps a small set of promo intents to hand-crafted SQL queries run against the local DuckDB mirror. The DatabricksGenieClient calls the real Genie Conversation API - start-conversation → poll messages/{id} → query-result - against Genie Spaces built over Unity Catalog tables.

Databricks Genie Spaces were made generally available in 2025 as domain-specific natural-language chat interfaces that sit over Unity Catalog data. A data analyst curates each space with datasets, example SQL queries, SQL expressions for business semantics, and terminology instructions. The result is a governed NL→SQL interface that respects Unity Catalog access controls at query time - which means a regional category manager can only see the stores and SKUs they're authorized to view, automatically, without any additional access management in Promograph itself.

For a Promograph deployment in a real retailer, three Genie Spaces would be created over the tables that already exist in most Databricks lakehouse environments:

# .env for full Databricks Genie deployment TPO_GENIE_BACKEND=databricks DATABRICKS_HOST=https://your-workspace.azuredatabricks.net DATABRICKS_TOKEN=dapi... DATABRICKS_WAREHOUSE_ID=abc123... # Three Genie Spaces over Unity Catalog GENIE_SPACE_SALES=... # Genie over transaction + basket data GENIE_SPACE_PROMO=... # Genie over promo calendar + event history GENIE_SPACE_INVENTORY=... # Genie over inventory + weeks-cover tables

That's the entire integration. One environment variable change, three Genie Spaces pointing at tables that any retail organization on Databricks already has, and Promograph has access to production data through a governed NL query layer - with every query logged, access-controlled, and auditable via Unity Catalog.

This is significant because Genie's most recent iteration (announced April 2026) now includes Agent Mode, MCP connections for external data sources, and native mobile apps for iOS and Android. A Promograph deployment built on Genie today would automatically inherit these capabilities - the category manager can query their promotional graph on their phone in the store aisle.

Unity Catalog as the Single Source of Truth

The dunnhumby-shaped synthetic data that Promograph generates locally mirrors the structure of the dunnhumby "Complete Journey" dataset that many retail Databricks customers already have in their Unity Catalog: product, promo_events, and sales tables with consistent schemas. Loading production data into Promograph is therefore straightforward:

# ETL: Unity Catalog → DuckDB mirror → Neo4j graph python data/etl/databricks_to_duckdb.py # pull via SQL connector, not Genie python -m backend.graph_store.build_edges # mine substitute + co-purchase edges python -m backend.graph_store.etl_duckdb_to_neo4j # populate Neo4j

The separation between ETL (SQL connector, for bulk data movement) and runtime query (Genie, for interactive NL questions) is architecturally sound. ETL does not consume Genie's rate limit (5 questions/minute on the free tier). At runtime, the orchestrator uses Genie for the specific, contextualized questions the LLM needs to answer - "What was the baseline velocity of SKU X in the last 4 weeks?" - which is exactly what Genie is designed for.

MLflow for Model Registry Integration

Promograph's GraphSAGE model and LightGBM forecaster are both logged as artifacts during training. In a production Databricks deployment, these would be registered in MLflow Model Registry with Unity Catalog-governed access. Model versions can be promoted from Staging to Production with approval workflows. The LightGBM model would be wrapped as a Feature Serving endpoint via Databricks Model Serving, eliminating the need to load model artifacts at API startup. This is not hypothetical - the model is already trained with PyTorch Geometric (which MLflow supports natively) and serialized in a format that MLflow can log and version.

Delta Live Tables for Graph Refresh

The substitute and co-purchase edge construction (build_edges.py) is a pure Python transformation over transaction data. In a production Databricks environment, this would be a Delta Live Tables pipeline that runs on a weekly schedule, refreshing the product relationship graph as new basket data arrives. The Neo4j graph would be a downstream sink of the DLT pipeline, rebuilt from the DuckDB mirror on each refresh. This turns graph construction from a manual ETL step into a governed, auditable, versioned data product.

Databricks Apps (launched in 2024) allows Python web applications to be deployed directly within a Databricks workspace, with single sign-on, Unity Catalog credential passthrough, and workspace-scoped networking. Promograph's FastAPI backend and React frontend are a textbook Databricks App candidate. The backend would run on a Databricks App compute with automatic credential forwarding to Genie and Unity Catalog. Retail Impact & Use Cases

6. What This Actually Means for Retailers

The academic and architectural case for graph-aware promotion optimization is strong. But what does this mean in practice, at the shelf-level, for a real retailer? Here are four concrete scenarios where Promograph's network-aware reasoning changes the outcome.

Scenario A: The Cannibal Promotion

A category manager at a mid-sized grocery chain wants to promote their private-label ground coffee at 30% off. A conventional TPO tool looks at historical price elasticity and predicts strong lift. It says go for it. Promograph queries the product graph and finds that this SKU has 7 substitute neighbours - all branded ground coffee variants - each with high edge weights indicating strong substitution. The GNN embedding places this SKU at the centre of a dense cluster. The LightGBM model, trained with cannibalization penalty in the target, predicts that the net incremental margin after accounting for full-margin branded ground coffee loss is negative at 30% off but marginally positive at 15% off. The pricing agent recommends 15%, not 30%, with a rationale citing two specific branded SKUs by name as the primary cannibalization risk. The category manager can click on those SKU nodes in the graph to see their substitute edge weights.

Scenario B: The Hidden Halo

The same retailer is considering promoting a premium recipe kit - a relatively low-velocity SKU with modest historical lift data. Conventional tools see limited uplift potential and deprioritize it. Promograph finds 12 co-purchase edges with high weights: the recipe kit is almost always bought alongside premium olive oil, fresh herbs, and a specific pasta brand. The halo pull from those co-purchases adds 34% to the net ROI estimate, pushing the recommendation from neutral to strongly positive. This halo insight is invisible to any system that doesn't know the basket-level co-purchase graph exists.

Scenario C: The Post-Promotion Dip Guard

A manager wants to repeat a promotion on a non-perishable item that performed well 6 weeks ago. Promograph checks days_since_last_promo in its feature set, finds it at 43 days, and flags that the post-promotion inventory stockpile from the previous event has likely not fully cleared. The LightGBM model, trained on the post-promotion demand trough pattern (documented extensively in Cohen et al. 2021), depresses the lift estimate accordingly and suggests waiting a further 3–4 weeks. This is a subtlety that requires temporal awareness of promo history, which Promograph encodes as a node type in the graph and a feature in the model.

Scenario D: The Overstocked Clearance

Inventory management flags that a seasonal confectionery SKU has 14 weeks of cover with 8 weeks of season remaining. The pricing agent's objective function adds a clearance_value term scaled by excess weeks cover, pushing the recommended discount depth higher than the net ROI formula alone would suggest. The model explicitly acknowledges it is recommending a below-optimal-margin discount for clearance reasons, and flags which specific halo neighbours (complementary confectionery, packaging items) will benefit from the promotional pull. The manager has full decision context, not just a number.

Technical Deep Dives

7. The Data Pipeline: From Basket Transactions to Graph Edges

Understanding how Promograph constructs its product relationship graph from raw transaction data is central to understanding its claims about cannibalization and halo effects. The pipeline has four stages.

Stage 1: Synthetic Data Generation (dunnhumby-shaped)

The backend/data_gen/generate.py module produces synthetic data that mirrors the schema of the dunnhumby "Complete Journey" dataset - one of the most widely used public retail transaction datasets in academic research. This is a deliberate choice: any organization that has loaded dunnhumby data into Unity Catalog (a common Databricks accelerator exercise) can replace the synthetic data with real data by pointing the ETL at their Unity Catalog tables. The synthetic schema includes products (sku, sub_commodity, brand, price), transactions (basket_id, household_id, sku, quantity, spend), and promo_events (sku, start_date, end_date, discount_pct, display_type).

Stage 2: Edge Mining

The build_edges.py script constructs three types of edges from the transaction data. Substitute edges connect products that share the same sub_commodity field and exhibit negative cross-elasticity: when A is promoted, B loses sales. Edge weights are computed from co-promotion analysis - the correlation between SKU A being promoted and SKU B's sales velocity declining. Co-purchase edges are mined from the basket data using Jaccard similarity over item co-occurrence: two items that appear together in baskets at a rate significantly above chance are connected with an edge weight reflecting that affinity. Promo interaction edges connect pairs of SKUs that have been promoted simultaneously within a rolling time window, capturing competitive promotional interference.

Stage 3: GraphSAGE Training

The train_graphsage.py script trains a 2-layer GraphSAGE model (PyTorch Geometric implementation) using an unsupervised link prediction objective over the substitute and co-purchase edges. The model learns to produce embeddings that place substitute neighbours close together and co-purchase pairs at predictable relative distances. The output is a 64-dimensional embedding for each SKU, persisted to data/embeddings.parquet. A numpy SVD fallback is available for environments without GPU or PyTorch Geometric, producing lower-quality but functional embeddings from the adjacency matrix directly.

Stage 4: LightGBM Forecaster Training

The train_lgbm.py script builds a training dataset where each row is a (SKU, promo_event) pair, features are the graph-derived + classical features described in Section 4, and the target is the observed net incremental margin over the promotion period. LightGBM is trained with early stopping on a time-based validation split (last 20% of promo events by date), preventing any lookahead leakage. The trained model is serialized and loaded at API startup.

# Quickstart: full pipeline from raw data to running API python -m backend.data_gen.generate # dunnhumby-shaped data → DuckDB python -m backend.graph_store.build_edges # mine 3 edge types from baskets python -m backend.gnn.train_graphsage # 64-dim SKU embeddings → parquet python -m backend.forecasting.train_lgbm # net ROI model → lgbm artifact # Smoke-test the full brain (no server needed) python -m backend.orchestrator.demo # Run the full stack uvicorn backend.api.app:app --port 8001 cd frontend && npm install && npm run dev # → http://localhost:5173
State of the Art Comparison

8. Where Promograph Sits in the Research Landscape

Promograph occupies a specific and underserved position in both the academic literature and the commercial software landscape. Mapping it precisely against related work reveals why the combination of its components is novel.

Versus Academic TPO Research

The Multi-POP formulation (Cohen et al. 2021) proves that pairwise cross-item effects are sufficient for optimality. Promograph implements this insight using GNN embeddings to learn the cross-item similarity structure from data rather than requiring it to be specified. This extends the theoretical framework to settings where substitution and complementarity relationships are not known in advance and must be mined from transaction history - the real-world case for any retailer with thousands of SKUs.

Versus Commercial TPO Vendors

SAP Trade Management, Oracle TPM, Anaplan, and IRI are all sophisticated systems, but their optimization engines are fundamentally regression models applied per-SKU or per-category. None of them, to the author's knowledge, use graph neural networks to encode product topology as a feature for lift prediction. The conversational interface is an emerging feature in some vendors (Anaplan has a Copilot layer; IRI has an AI assistant) but none expose the product relationship graph interactively as part of the reasoning interface. The ability to click a node and steer the model's next reasoning turn is genuinely novel in this space.

Versus General GNN Research

The GNN literature has extensive work on recommendation systems (PinSage, LightGCN), knowledge graph completion (TransE, RotatE), and social network analysis. Retail promotion optimization is a distinctly different task - it requires predicting causal intervention effects (what happens if I change the price?) rather than static node properties or link probabilities. The use of GNN embeddings as features for a downstream causal-like prediction model, combined with a constrained optimization agent, is a pragmatic and production-appropriate adaptation of GNN methodology to a domain where it has not been widely applied.

Research Gap Promograph Addresses

The combination of three things in one open-source system is novel: (1) graph-aware promotion impact prediction using inductive GNN embeddings, (2) net ROI as the optimization target - not gross uplift - explicitly accounting for cannibalization and halo, and (3) a conversational interface where the product graph is the reasoning medium, not just a visualization. None of the academic papers cited here implement all three; none of the commercial tools implement any of them in this combination.

What Comes Next

9. Open Questions and What a Production System Needs

Promograph is a complete working system, not a research prototype. But honest assessment requires naming what a production deployment at a real retailer would need to add.

Benchmark Numbers

The system currently lacks published benchmark results on the synthetic dataset: what is the LightGBM model's mean absolute error on net ROI prediction? How much does the GraphSAGE embedding improve prediction accuracy over a no-embedding baseline? These numbers should be computable on the synthetic data and would substantially strengthen the empirical case for the GNN component. The GraphSAGE vs. numpy SVD fallback comparison is particularly interesting - quantifying the embedding quality degradation would validate (or challenge) the GraphSAGE choice.

Elasticity Estimation

The current pricing agent uses a simplified price elasticity estimate. In production, elasticity should be estimated from historical promo response data using a demand model that accounts for the endogeneity of promotional pricing - prices are not randomly assigned, so naive regression estimates of elasticity are biased. Double Machine Learning (Chernozhukov et al. 2018, the DML framework recently covered by Microsoft's EconML) is the state-of-the-art approach to debiased elasticity estimation and would be a natural upgrade to the pricing agent.

Multi-Store and Multi-Region Graphs

The current graph is built globally over all transactions. In a large retailer, the product relationship graph differs across store formats (hypermarket vs. convenience), geographies (urban vs. rural), and seasons. A production system would maintain segmented graphs and serve embeddings appropriate to the store context of each query. This is a tractable extension of the current architecture - it requires partitioning the graph building step by store segment and adding a store_segment parameter to the subgraph query.

Real-Time Graph Updates

Co-purchase relationships evolve as consumer behaviour changes, new products launch, and seasonal patterns shift. The current pipeline rebuilds the entire graph from scratch. A production system would use incremental graph updates - streaming basket data into the co-purchase edge computation and updating edge weights using a decaying average that weights recent transactions more heavily. This is where Databricks Delta Live Tables with streaming sources becomes particularly valuable.

10. Conclusion: The Promotion Problem Is a Graph Problem

The trade promotion industry has spent decades trying to solve a fundamentally relational problem with fundamentally non-relational tools. Single-SKU regression models cannot see cannibalization because they cannot represent the substitution graph. They cannot see halo effects because they cannot represent the co-purchase graph. They cannot explain their recommendations because there is no graph to show.

Promograph does not try to replace every component of enterprise TPM software. It targets the specific gap that costs retailers the most money: the failure to account for network-wide revenue effects when making single-SKU promotion decisions. By representing the product catalogue as a graph, encoding that graph structure into SKU embeddings with GraphSAGE, and targeting net ROI across the entire neighbourhood rather than gross uplift on the promoted SKU, it produces a different kind of recommendation - one that is simultaneously more accurate, more explainable, and more actionable.

The Databricks integration is not cosmetic. Genie Spaces, Unity Catalog, Delta Live Tables, MLflow, and Databricks Apps together provide the governed, production-grade data infrastructure that transforms a local demonstration into an enterprise deployment. Every design choice in Promograph - the mockable Genie client, the DuckDB fallback, the inductive GraphSAGE, the typed SSE events - was made with that deployment path in mind.

The project is open source, runs at zero infrastructure cost, and is available at github.com/dcsgod/promograph. The dunnhumby-compatible data schema means any organization already running retail analytics on Databricks can connect it to production data with a handful of environment variables and an afternoon of Genie Space configuration.

The promotion problem is a graph problem. The tools to solve it now exist. What remains is applying them.
